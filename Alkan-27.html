ALKAN-27

Alkan-27 addresses the challenge of limited recycling facilities through a sound-based waste sorting mechanism. This system incentivizes users to recycle aluminum cans by rewarding them with monetary incentives, thus promoting recycling and enhancing accessibility, particularly in remote areas.

Problem
Purpose
Objective
Limited nearby recycling facilities pose a challenge for aluminum can recycling. The lack of easy access to these centers results in improper disposal of aluminum cans in regular waste bins, impeding effective waste management and hindering the efficient reuse of aluminum. This has a negative impact on the environment and our recycling efforts.
Address the problem of limited or lack of nearby recycling facilities and promote effective aluminum can recycling, facilitate proper sorting and recycling of aluminum cans, reduce waste and minimize environmental impact, and provide an accessible and cost-effective solution, encouraging sustainable recycling practices and maximizing the reuse of valuable aluminum resources.
Our objective is to develop an affordable device that utilizes sound signatures to distinguish aluminum cans from waste. The device should stay within a budget of $500 while maintaining a minimum success rate of 50% in accurately identifying aluminum cans. It should also accommodate various sizes of aluminum cans, enabling efficient recycling practices.

Design Process

Observations: Identified potential factors contributing to low recycling rates in the community and devised potential solutions.

Waste categorizer: a device that automatically categorizes waste into recyclables, landfill, compost, hazardous waste, etc., to save time.

Aluminum deposit: a portable device that serves as a place for people to deposit aluminum cans and receive monetary incentives.

To better understand the reasons behind low recycling rates, I conducted a global survey. Surprisingly, the primary cause was the lack of access to nearby recycling facilities. This finding led us to choose the second idea.


When selecting components for Alkan-27, simplicity and accuracy were prioritized. Two primary sensors were considered: a camera and a microphone. While the camera interface offered visual recognition capabilities, it introduced complexity and required a significant amount of development time. On the other hand, a microphone simplifies the system and reduces complexity, albeit with the potential drawback of sensitivity to ambient noise. To meet project deadlines and balance simplicity with accuracy, the microphone was choosen as the primary sensor.

The mechanism is designed to analyze the sound of an object hitting the plate. If it detects the sound of an aluminum can, the plate pivots towards the recycling side, simultaneously activating the coin dispensing system for users to collect coins. Conversely, if the item is not an aluminum can, the plate pivots towards the trash side without activating the coin dispensing system. This functionality is achieved through a combination of a microphone, servos, potentiometer, echolocation sensor, and an Arduino Uno.

Incentive Process

Prototype 1

Demo

The initial iteration consisted of a low fidelity prototype made out of cardboard, including the rotating plate. It contains a microphone, Arduino Uno, servo, and micro servo. The microphone captures the sound signatures, while the Arduino Uno processes data and manages system operation. The servo directs the rotating plate, guiding cans to the recycling bin. The micro servo activates the coin dispensing system, when an aluminum can is deposited.

Test Results

Tests were conducted using various objects (aluminum cans, Styrofoam cups, etc.). The prototype performed better when the mechanism was outside the box. This could be attributed to factors such as heat accumulation, sensor sensitivity, and sound variations within the enclosure. The second prototype underwent modifications to address these issues.

Prototype 2

To address heat accumulation, sensor sensitivity, and sound variations within the enclosure, a potentiometer was incorporated for fine-tuning purposes. An echolocation sensor was also added to detect objects with minimal sound, such as napkins. Additionally, the housing was rebuilt using wood to provide increased support for the carousel and coin dispensing system. Hinged doors were added for easy access to the recyclables. To improve sound recogniton, the cardboard plate was replaced by a metal one. Furthermore, the dimensions of the enclosure were thoughtfully selected to maintain portability without compromising functionality.


Demo

In this iteration, the enclosure was rebuilt, incorporating additional components to enhance functionality. Alongside the existing components from the initial iteration, a potentiometer and echolocation sensor were integrated. During the demonstration, the echolocation and microphone sensors were tested using a napkin, an item that produces minimal sound upon deposit into the machine. However, overall performance improved significantly compared to that of the initial iteration.

Test Results

Despite the integration of a potentiometer for fine-tuning, ambient noise from the surroundings and sensors within the enclosure continued to impact performance. Accuracy remained higher when the components were positioned outside the enclosure, indicating that further refinements are necessary to optimize performance.
